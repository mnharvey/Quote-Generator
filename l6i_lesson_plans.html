Inkscape · Lesson 1
Knowing the Tool
Introduction to the Inkscape interface — toolbars, panels, canvas navigation, and how to get around efficiently.
Top Toolbar Setup
→Build your right toolbar with the tools you use most: Arrow (select), Align & Distribute, Trace Bitmap, Delete, Find & Replace, Fill & Stroke, and Export.
→Export tool tip: click Export and change DPI to 300 for print-ready files.
Document & Canvas Setup
→Show Document Page, Selection, and Custom tabs. Hit Selection to work on the canvas. Note: Selection may not activate until an object is on the canvas.
→Coordinates X and Y start at the top left corner of the canvas.
→Units are typically mm but can be changed to inches in the top bar. Lock the box to keep proportions — unlocked can skew or distort objects. This applies to all objects.
Zooming & Navigation
→Laptop: Two fingers to zoom — pinch/spread centers on cursor. Slide two fingers left/right to pan.
→Desktop: Ctrl + scroll wheel to zoom. Shift + scroll to move left/right.
Left Panel — Main Tools
→Arrow tool — main selection tool for moving and resizing objects.
→Node tool — shows the nodes that make up a shape. Nodes can be moved to reshape an image.
→Shape tools — squares, circles, star/polygon. Make shapes from the left toolbar. Double-clicking on the line of a star/polygon while on that tool lets you change the number of points or corners.
→Text tool (A) — click and drag to create a text box. Use the arrow tool to resize the box — width and height show in the top bar. After typing, click the arrow button to exit the text box.
→We don't use shape builder, but we do use circle, square, and text tools regularly.
Text & Fonts
→Font bar is at the top — change font after you have your text selected. Fonts available are based on what is installed on your device.
→Extensions > Text > Change Case — convert text to ALL UPPERCASE or lowercase. Very useful for uniform name printing.
→Split Text — paste a long list of names and it will split each name out individually while keeping the original. Great for name lists.
Layers, Ordering & Alignment
→Overlap 3 shapes to practice. Top toolbar buttons: Raise to Top, Raise One Step, Lower One Step, Lower to Bottom.
→Align & Distribute (right toolbar): set "Relative to" > Selection to center objects to each other. Use "Relative to Page" to center to the full canvas.
→Select multiple objects by dragging a box around them, or hold Shift and click each one.
→Distribute: "Make horizontal/vertical gaps equal" is the most used — great for evenly spacing labels or repeated elements.
Hotkeys & Shortcuts
→Ctrl+D — Duplicate (creates a copy directly on top of the original)
→Ctrl+C / Ctrl+V — Copy and Paste (may need Shift instead of Ctrl depending on Inkscape version)
→Ctrl+G — Group (may be Shift+G depending on version)
→Ctrl+U — Ungroup (same version variation applies)
→Backspace — Delete selected object
→Rotate 90° and Flip tools are available in the top bar.
Additional Tips * External
→* Inkscape's built-in tutorials are a great reference: Help menu > Tutorials > Basic / Shapes / Advanced. External
→* To clean up a document, you can clear unused gradients, patterns, and markers via File > Clean Up Document. External
→* Hold Shift while resizing to resize from center. Hold Ctrl while resizing to keep aspect ratio locked. External
Inkscape · Lesson 2
Fill vs Stroke
Understanding the difference between fill (inside color) and stroke (outline/border) and how to control each independently.
The Basics
→Fill = the inner color of a shape. Stroke = the line/border around it.
→All colors are shown along the bottom of the screen. Right-click a color to choose "Set Fill" or "Set Stroke."
Right Panel — Fill & Stroke Controls
→Stroke Style — controls the line itself: width, dashed lines (useful for cut lines on stickers), line caps and joins.
→Stroke Paint — changes the color of the stroke. Can switch between RGB and CMYK at the top.
→To remove a fill or stroke color: the far left box (X) removes it. The solid box next to it gives a flat solid color. The remaining options are gradients.
Gradients
→The gradient bar lets you set multiple colors and control how they blend using the slider/Stop color tool.
→The Stop color controls where one color ends and the next begins. You can adjust hues for each stop independently.
→Same gradient options are available in both the Fill and Stroke sections.
Stroke for Sticker Cut Lines
→Stroke is especially important when creating sticker cut lines. More detail on this will be covered in the Offset lesson.
Additional Tips * External
→* You can switch between RGB and CMYK color modes at the top of the Fill & Stroke panel. Always use CMYK for print production files. External
→* To quickly remove fill or stroke: select the object and press Shift+Ctrl+F to open Fill & Stroke, then click the X icon. External
Inkscape · Lesson 3
Changing Colors — Black & White Conversion
How to convert a design to black and white or apply color changes using Inkscape's Filters menu. Uses the PNG exported from the Canva lesson as practice.
Importing Your Design
→Import the file using File > Import.
→Check the sizing at the top bar — it should match but always worth verifying on any design.
→You can move the design around. Sometimes working off the canvas is easier depending on what you're doing.
Fade to Black or White
→Select the design, then go to Filters > Colors > Fade to Black or White.
→Since the design is black, it will ask to fade to white. Use a low value like 0.03, then click Apply.
Colorize — Adding Color to a Design
→Go to Filters > Colors > Colorize for color-tinting options.
→Enable Live Preview to see changes in real time as you adjust.
→Under the Options tab: set Blend 1 to Normal and Blend 2 to Normal. Then adjust the color sliders to get your desired hue.

Inkscape · Lesson 5
Cropping — Clipping & Masking
How to crop images in Inkscape using the Clip tool. Most important for DTF file prep — ensures your design is the right size and cleanly cropped.
Step-by-Step: Setting a Clip
→Select the object — you will see a gap between the dotted line and the edge of the image.
→Draw a filled rectangle box over the design. Remove the stroke from the box.
→Move the box to the background and size it as close to the design as possible without cutting out any details.
→Select the main design and send it to the back.
→Select both the design and the box.
→Go to Object > Clip > Set. Make sure units are set to inches at the top.
After Clipping
→Resize to confirm the design is the correct size. Make sure the lock is on to keep proportions.
→Made a mistake? Ctrl+Z to undo.
→This is the most important lesson for DTF file prep.
Inkscape · Lesson 6
Transparency — Opacity & Blending
Understanding transparency in Inkscape — mostly about making sure your fill settings are correct and knowing when transparency helps vs hurts.
The Main Rule
→For production files, mostly focus on making sure your fill is correct. Transparency should usually stay at 100%.
→In the CMYK fill color panel, the A value controls transparency. Do not adjust this — keep it at 100% for production files.
Transparency as a Cropping Aid
→You can temporarily use transparency to help with cropping — instead of sending the box to the background (as in Lesson 5), you can fade it to see the design underneath.
→Important: if you use transparency this way, you must reset it back to 100% before clipping and exporting. Leaving transparency on will affect the final file.
Inkscape · Lesson 7
Tracing & Vectorizing — Bitmap Trace
Converting a raster image (JPEG) into a scalable vector using Inkscape's Trace Bitmap tool. Once vectorized, the design can be colored, exported as SVG/PDF/PNG, and edited with nodes.
Preparing the File
→Go back to Canva and re-download the same design — this time as JPEG.
→JPEG never has a transparent background — it will always have a white background. That's expected here.
→Size doesn't matter for this step.
→Delete the old file from the previous lesson in Inkscape, then import the JPEG.
Running the Trace
→Click the image, then access Trace Bitmap from the toolbar or by right-clicking the object.
→Detection mode: Brightness Cutoff — senses colors and converts to one solid color.
→Adjust the threshold: black is sensed first, light yellow last. Lighter colors need a higher threshold. Adjust and hit Apply.
→After applying, you will have a transparent vectorized design — the white background is removed.
Working with the Vector
→The design is now a vector — you can change its color, export as SVG, PDF, PNG, and more.
→Select the Node tool to see nodes. Nodes tell the laser where to cut and the printer where to print.
→You can delete nodes and adjust them to clean up the image. Deleting inside nodes can convert a detailed area into a solid fill.
Sizing & Rotating
→Drag corners to resize proportionally. Drag the sides to squish or squeeze (use lock to avoid this).
→Double-click the object to switch to rotate mode — drag corners to rotate.
Additional Tips * External
→* For best trace results, start with a high-contrast, clean image. Low-res or blurry images will produce messy node paths that are hard to clean up. External
→* After tracing, always delete the original raster image underneath — the trace sits on top of it and you don't want both in your file. External
→* Use Path > Simplify (Ctrl+L) after tracing to reduce the number of nodes and create cleaner, smoother paths. External
Inkscape · Lesson 8
Creating an Offset — Outlines & Borders
How to create an offset or border around a vectorized design — used for sticker cut lines, contour lines, and outline effects. Two methods covered.
Before You Start
→Offsets are for sticker outlines, contour cut lines, and border effects.
→You can only create an offset on a vectorized image. If the design has nodes, it's vectorized and ready.
Method 1 — Linked Offset (Path Menu)
→Go to Path > Linked Offset. This creates a second layer around the design.
→Click and hold the diamond handle that appears, then drag it away from the design to grow the offset.
→Go back to Path > Union to convert the outline into its own vector shape.
→Now you can delete inside nodes to create a solid filled object. You may need to click the arrow tool to exit node mode first.
→Change the color and you now have a clean outline/border around your design.
Method 2 — Stroke Style
→Go to Fill & Stroke on the right panel. Hit Stroke Paint and change it to a flat color fill.
→Go to Stroke Style and make the width very large to create a visible border.
→Duplicate the design and delete the stroke from the duplicate to keep the original clean. This adds depth to the design.
→Note: this method has limitations — you cannot edit gaps the same way you can with node editing in Method 1.

Canva · Lesson 2
Creating a Design
Starting a design from scratch — setting canvas size, adding elements, and exporting for use in other tools like Inkscape.
Getting Started
→Hit Create to start a new design.
→Size matters — always go bigger than you need, it's easier to size down. Example: business card at 5×7 inches. Banners may need more precise dimensions. We use inches.
→Title your design so it doesn't get lost in your project list.
Left Toolbar Overview
→Templates — pre-made Canva layouts to start from.
→Elements — clip art, shapes, graphics, and more.
→Text — add text boxes to your design.
→Branding — covered in the next lesson.
→Canva AI — AI-assisted design help.
→Uploads — where you bring in outside images like client logos.
Practice Exercise (links to Inkscape Lesson 3)
→Select Text from the toolbar and type a message (e.g. "Matt is Great"). Size it on the canvas.
→Select a font. Keep it black — important for the Inkscape color lesson that follows.
→Add a simple element from the Elements toolbar. Keep it black as well.
→When done, click Share > Download. Select PNG, set the size as large as possible, enable Transparent Background, then hit Download.
→This exported PNG will be used in Inkscape to practice the color-changing lesson.
Additional Tips * External
→* To select an element that's hidden behind another, hold Ctrl (or Cmd) and click the top layer, then keep clicking to cycle through layers underneath. External
→* Drag a text box to the full width of the canvas to auto-center text horizontally without needing the alignment tool. External
→* Duplicate text boxes (Ctrl+D) to keep the same font and size on a new line — then just change the words. External

Canva · Lesson 4
Remove Backgrounds
Using Canva's BG Remover tool to remove white or unwanted backgrounds from images — especially useful for AI-generated designs that may not have a true transparent background.
Getting Started
→Add your image to the Canva page.
→Many AI-generated designs are not crisp and may not have a truly transparent background even if they appear to.
→To check: click the canvas background and change its color. If the image background changes color with it, the image is NOT transparent.
Using BG Remover
→With the image selected, hit BG Remover in the top bar.
→If it removes too much or needs areas restored, hit BG again to enter the editing studio.
→In the studio, use the Restore brush to bring back areas that were incorrectly removed, or use the Erase brush to remove areas that should have been deleted.
→Adjust the brush size depending on the level of detail needed.
→If you make mistakes, you can go back — but you may need to reset and start the removal again.
→Use the Preview option to see what has changed before applying.
Things to Know
→Some designs work great with Canva BG Remover. Others need more work or may be better handled in a different tool.
→Always export as PNG after removal to preserve the transparent background.
Additional Tips * External
→* For complex or detailed images where Canva BG Remover struggles, try remove.bg (free online tool) then import the result back into Canva. External
→* Change the canvas background to a bright color like hot pink before removing backgrounds — this makes it much easier to spot leftover white areas. External
Canva · Lesson 5
Edits — Outlines, Color Changes & Adjustments
Adding outlines and effects to designs in Canva — useful for creating sticker-ready artwork with visible borders. Two methods covered.
Before You Start
→To create a sticker from your design, you can group all elements together first, or select individual elements to apply effects to.
Method 1 — Shadow as Outline
→Select an element or grouped design.
→Go to Edit and scroll all the way down to Shadows. Click See All.
→Select the Outline option from the shadow styles.
→Change the shadow/outline color — magenta works well as a sticker cut line color.
→Adjust the size of the outline to your preference.
→Note: some designs will work better than others with this method.
Method 2 — New Page with Original PNG
→In Canva, click Add Page to create a new canvas.
→Re-import your original PNG design onto the new page.
→Repeat the Shadow/Outline steps from Method 1.
→This is useful when you want to keep the original design clean on one page and the outlined version on another.
Canva · Lesson 6
Making Mockups
Creating product mockups in Canva to show clients how their design will look — using Canva photos or pulling real product images from supplier sites.
Method 1 — Canva Photos
→In the left toolbar, go to Elements — instead of Graphics, switch to Photos.
→Search for and pick a product photo that matches your design (e.g. a blank t-shirt).
→Place your design on top of the product photo and adjust size and position.
Method 2 — Pull from a Supplier Site
→Go to companycasuals.com (or similar supplier site).
→Search for the exact shirt style and color your customer is ordering. Right-click the image and copy it.
→Go back to Canva and paste the image directly onto your canvas.
→Now place your design on top of the shirt image.
Adjusting the Design for the Mockup
→Click your design, go to Edit > Filters to change colors as needed.
→To flip a black design to white: go to Edit > Adjust and scroll about halfway down to find the Invert option.
Adding a QR Code to a Mockup or Flyer
→If your mockup or flyer needs a QR code, go to Apps in the left side menu.
→Search for and click the QR Code app.
→Click Gen QR and enter the URL you want the QR code to link to.
→Click Add to Design and position it on your canvas.
Additional Tips * External
→* Use Canva's Smart Mockup feature (Pro) to drag and drop your design directly onto product photos and it auto-adjusts perspective. External
→* For color accuracy on shirt mockups, always check the actual product color name against the supplier spec sheet before sending to the client. External
→* Placeit.net is a great alternative for apparel mockups if Canva's options are limited — offers many branded shirt styles and colors. External
Canva · Lesson 7
Exporting — PNG vs PDF vs JPEG
Choosing the right export format matters — the wrong choice can make a file unusable on our equipment. Here's what to use and when.
PNG
→Best for DTF/t-shirts — export as the largest size with a transparent background.
→Also used for stickers, logos, and any design that needs a transparent background.
→PNG files will NOT go into the flatbed printer — use PDF for flatbed.
JPEG
→Use JPEG only when the design does not need any transparency or extra design layers — foam boards, banners, corrugated signs.
→JPEG never has a transparent background.
PDF
→PDF works with most of our machines.
→Always use PDF Print — not PDF Standard — and select CMYK.
→RGB is for digital screens. CMYK is for printing. Always use CMYK for production files.
→In CorelDRAW, you can extract the image from the background of a PDF.
→PDF Standard can be used for paper-based items.
SVG
→SVG works in the laser — but the design must be a solid color for the laser to read it correctly.
Additional Tips * External
→* When exporting multiple pages in Canva as PDF, you can choose "All Pages" or select specific pages — useful when only certain pages are ready for print. External
→* PNG files are larger in file size than JPEG. If emailing a mockup to a client for preview only, JPEG is fine and will send faster. External
→* SVG export is a Canva Pro feature. On the free plan, download as PDF and open in Inkscape to convert if needed. External
